# Deliberate Activity — Section 1: Scope and Applicability

## Round 1

### Consensus Preference: "Risk-Proportionate Scope"

These guidelines apply to any procurement decision where the product or service uses machine learning, neural networks, or other trained models to generate outputs that influence organisational decisions. This includes direct AI procurement (purchasing an AI product), indirect AI procurement (purchasing a product with embedded AI features), and AI-as-a-service (using AI capabilities through APIs or cloud services).

The level of assessment required is proportionate to the potential impact. Procurement decisions are categorised as high-impact (affecting decisions about people, safety, or legal compliance), medium-impact (affecting business operations or strategy), or low-impact (informational or efficiency-focused). High-impact decisions require full assessment against all risk categories. Medium-impact decisions require targeted assessment. Low-impact decisions require only vendor disclosure and basic documentation.

### Popular Preference: "Broad Disclosure, Simple Assessment"

These guidelines apply to any product or service that incorporates artificial intelligence or machine learning capabilities, regardless of whether AI is the primary function or an embedded feature. The scope is intentionally broad to prevent AI capabilities from evading assessment by being bundled into larger products.

For all in-scope procurements, organisations should require vendors to complete a standardised AI disclosure form covering: what AI/ML is used, what data it was trained on, what decisions it influences, and what transparency is available. This disclosure is the foundation. Based on the disclosure, procurement teams apply a single assessment framework, spending more time on higher-risk items but using the same structure throughout.

Personal productivity tools chosen by individual employees (e.g., AI writing assistants, browser extensions) are out of scope for procurement guidelines and should be addressed through acceptable use policies instead.

### Anti-Consensus Preference: "Vendor Accountability, Not Buyer Assessment"

These guidelines take a fundamentally different approach: rather than asking procurement teams to assess AI (which they lack the expertise to do well), the responsibility shifts to vendors. Any vendor selling products to consortium members must self-certify against a responsible AI standard, provide ongoing transparency reports, and accept contractual liability for AI-related harms.

Procurement teams don't need to understand AI deeply. They need to verify that the vendor has completed self-certification, check the transparency report for red flags, and ensure the contract includes standard AI liability clauses. This is similar to how procurement handles information security today — buyers check for SOC 2 certification rather than auditing security practices themselves.

The scope is simple: if the vendor markets or documents AI/ML capabilities in their product, these contractual requirements apply. If they don't disclose AI and it's later discovered, they're in breach.

**Participant A:**
Only a 4/10 from me. I get the appeal — push complexity to vendors. But this doesn't work for two reasons. First, there's no universal "responsible AI standard" to self-certify against. Who creates it? Who audits it? Second, vendor self-certification is notoriously unreliable. SOC 2 works because there are independent auditors. There's no equivalent for AI ethics.
